Don Quixote (Alonso Quixano) — a middle-aged hidalgo of La Mancha who believes himself a knight-errant; the hero
Sancho Panza — Quixote's loyal, proverb-quoting squire; lured by the promise of his own island to govern; rides a donkey
Dulcinea del Toboso (Aldonza Lorenzo) — a young country girl whom Quixote imagines as his noble lady
Rocinante — Don Quixote's frail, elderly horse
Dapple — Sancho Panza's loyal donkey
Cide Hamete Benegeli — the fictional Moorish historian whom Cervantes credits with writing the story; a satiric figure accused of dishonesty
Samson Carrasco — a young student from Quixote's village who disguises himself as the Knight of the Mirrors and Knight of the White Moon to force Quixote home
The Priest (Pedro Pérez) — a good friend of Quixote who conspires with the Barber to bring him to his senses
The Barber (Maese Nicolás) — another friend of Quixote; conspires with the Priest to bring him home
A Barber (unnamed) — wearing a basin on his head as protection from rain; attacked by Quixote who believes it to be the helmet of Mambrino
Teresa Panza — Sancho's sensible wife; runs the household while Sancho is away
The Duke and Duchess — decadent nobles who mock Quixote and Sancho with elaborate pranks
Cardenio — a young man driven mad when his lover Lucinda marries Don Fernando; reunited with her at the end of Book I
Dorothea — deceived by Don Fernando; disguises herself as Princess Micomicona to win Quixote's help; conspires with Cardenio
Ginés de Pasamonte — a violent criminal freed by Quixote who steals Sancho's donkey; later reappears as Master Pedro
John Haldudo, the Rich — castigated by Quixote for beating his servant-boy in Quixote's first adventure
Andrew — a young apprentice whom Quixote attempts to help, causing more trouble
Antonia — Quixote's loving niece; conflicted about her uncle's chivalric preoccupation
Muñaton — a scholar whom Antonia accuses of stealing Quixote's library
Friston — the sage enchanter Quixote blames for stealing his library and transforming giants into windmills
Marcella — a beautiful young shepherdess who refuses to be married and lives in the wild
Lucinda — the woman Cardenio hoped to marry; wed Don Fernando to appease her parents; truly loves Cardenio
Don Fernando — betrays his friend Cardenio by marrying Lucinda; has also broken a promise to marry Dorothea
Tinacrio the Wise and Queen Xaramilla — father and mother of the Princess Micomicona
Innkeeper #1 — performs the dubbing ceremony in which Quixote receives his knightly name
Innkeeper #2 — an innkeeper Quixote patronises, believing his inn to be a castle
Innkeeper #2's daughter — a beautiful young woman Quixote takes for a princess
Maritornes — a nearly blind, hunchbacked woman who works at Inn #2
The Canon — a religious figure who once tried writing chivalric tales; marvels at Quixote's blend of lucidity and foolishness
Holy Brotherhood Officer — intends to arrest Quixote for freeing galley slaves; dissuaded by the Priest on account of Quixote's insanity
Galley slaves — violent criminals freed by Quixote; beat him and escape when asked to present themselves to Dulcinea
